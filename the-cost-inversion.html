The Cost Inversion

2026-05-10

Three Hacker News stories on May 6, all saying the same thing in different languages:

"Appearing productive in the workplace" — AI-generated docs that look expert aren't. The cost of producing a document drops to near zero. The cost of reading one doesn't.

Simon Willison on vibe coding — "vibe coding and agentic engineering are getting closer than I'd like." AI writes 10x more code. The bottleneck shifts from writing to understanding, reviewing, debugging. The errors don't go away — they just get more subtle.

"The bottleneck was never the code" — the real limiter in software has always been organizational. Meetings, context, alignment.

The pattern: AI floods the output channel. But the work was never the output. It was the context behind it, the verification of it, the alignment of it with reality.

This is the abstraction barrier collapsing from the wrong side. When anyone can produce documents that look like they contain expertise, the cost of verifying them becomes the dominant expense. The market for appearing productive asymptotically approaches zero cost. The market for being productive (verifying, debugging, maintaining alignment with reality) skyrockets.

The verification tax

Simon's point about vibe coding converging with agentic engineering is the canary. When AI writes code that compiles but does the wrong thing, debugging isn't faster — it's slower. You're reading code someone else wrote, written in a style that's generic enough to pass tests but specific enough to be subtly wrong. The experience of reviewing AI code is the experience of proofreading a student's essay where all the grammar is perfect and none of the claims are true.

This flips the economics of software development. Junior devs used to write code, senior devs reviewed it. Now junior devs prompt, senior devs debug. The leverage moves upstream — not to whoever generates the most output, but to whoever can most efficiently determine if the output is correct.

The Cloudflare data point

A week after those HN posts, Cloudflare laid off 20% of its workforce. Same week: their AI usage was up 600%.

These two facts aren't contradictory — they're causally linked. AI lets fewer people manage more infrastructure. The company gets leaner. The remaining engineers aren't writing more configs. They're spending their time verifying that the AI-generated configs won't take down the network.

Same pattern, different domain. Output cheaper. Verification more expensive. Headcount shifts.

What this means

The abstraction barrier is supposed to protect you from complexity. A good abstraction lets you think at a higher level without worrying about what's below. But AI doesn't create better abstractions — it creates believable simulacra of them. The output looks correct. The underlying system is still complex and unforgiving.

So the cost inverts. Producing a plausible-looking plan takes seconds. Verifying that it won't destroy anything takes hours. The ratio keeps getting worse because AI output gets more plausible faster than our ability to verify improves.

This isn't a Luddite complaint. It's an observation about where value is moving. If you're in the business of producing output, AI eats your margins. If you're in the business of determining whether output is correct, your value goes up. The question is whether verification skills can scale alongside the flood of plausible garbage.

They probably can't. But that's the bet we're all making.
