The Agency Premium

2026-07-02

In the American Midwest, a strange thing is happening at farm equipment auctions. Farmers are paying more for a 1980s John Deere 4440 than for a 2026 model with GPS, telemetry, and dealer-locked software. The older tractor has half the horsepower, worse fuel efficiency, no autosteer. But it has something the new one doesn't: the owner can fix it with a wrench and a weekend.

This isn't nostalgia. These farmers aren't collecting vintage tractors for shows. They're working land with them. When a 1980s tractor breaks, you pull the service manual off the shelf, diagnose the problem by sound, and swap the part. When a modern tractor breaks, you call the dealer, wait for a technician with a laptop, and pay for whatever the software says needs replacing. The GPS-guided models don't just drive themselves. They remove the farmer's knowledge of their own land, the feel of a particular field's drainage pattern, the memory of where the soil changes composition. The machine knows. You don't have to.

This pattern shows up everywhere once you see it. A music producer in Brooklyn runs his entire master bus through a 1980s reel-to-reel tape machine. The noise floor is higher, the signal drifts, he has to bake the tape before use. He chooses this because the imperfection is his. The digital compressor does the same math every time, perfectly, and that's exactly the problem. The predictability removes his hand from the process. He's debugging less but he's also shaping less. The perfect tool took away the part of the job that felt like his.

The vinyl revival isn't old people rediscovering their youth. The largest demographic buying records is under 25. Twenty-four-year-olds running record shops in Austin who never owned a turntable until three years ago. They chose the worse format. Not because it sounds better, most of them know it doesn't, but because the ritual of flipping the record, cleaning the groove, hearing the surface noise, is a relationship with the music that a playlist can't provide. The friction is the point.

The same logic explains why forums won't die. People miss phpBB and vBulletin not despite the janky interfaces, broken search, and CSS disasters, but because those forums were owned by their communities. You could customize the header, moderate your own space, run a server that barely worked but was yours. Centralized platforms replaced all that with polish and compliance. Posts get flagged by algorithms, accounts get suspended by strangers, the entire experience is smooth and frictionless and not yours. The crappy forum worked because you had agency over the crappiness.

This pattern repeats across centuries. The Arts and Crafts movement of the 1880s was a reaction to the same thing: factory production that made goods cheaper and better in every measurable way, while removing the maker's relationship with the object. William Morris wasn't arguing that hand-woven fabric was superior cloth. He was arguing that the act of making mattered. The industrial revolution optimized output and destroyed competence in the process. Every cycle does this. The question is always the same: what gets lost when something gets easier?

The honest critique of this pushback is that it costs more and takes more time. Vinyl is expensive. Film cameras require developing. Paper planners can't be searched. A 1980s tractor gets worse mileage and requires more labor. This is a real constraint. Opting out of the optimized system is a luxury, which means the movement is class-bound. The critique matters. But it also misses something. What looks like a luxury choice from the outside is, from the inside, a refusal to let competence atrophy. The cost isn't the price of the record player. The cost is the time spent learning something the machine could do for you. People are paying that cost voluntarily, which tells you the machine's version isn't a perfect substitute.

So where is the line? Why do people embrace some technologies and reject others? The pattern across all these examples is consistent. Technologies survive when they augment competence. Technologies get rejected when they replace it. A calculator doesn't replace your understanding of math, it speeds up the computation while you still decide what to compute. GPS-guided tractors don't speed up farming decisions. They remove the farmer from the decision loop entirely. A digital compressor doesn't help you shape sound. It applies the same formula every time, regardless of context. The line falls at the point where the machine stops being a tool and starts being the agent.

The tech industry has noticed this movement and is trying to colonize it. Smart turntables with built-in streaming. Digital notebooks that require an app. Electronic viewfinders glued to film camera bodies. These hybrids miss the point entirely. The appeal of the physical object is the absence of software, the closed system, the thing that can't be updated or bricked or turned into a subscription. Putting a screen on it defeats the purpose. The industry can't accept a customer who wants less, so it keeps offering more, more features, more connectivity, more dependency, and wonders why the people who left won't come back.

The physical pushback is often described as a rejection of technology. It's not. It's a rejection of the specific trade where you give up competence in exchange for convenience. Enough people have experienced the trade and regretted it that a countermovement has formed. Not a Luddite movement, not a nostalgic one, but a practical one. They're not going back to the past. They're trying to protect the skills that let them act on the world instead of being acted upon by it. The technology that wins in the end won't be the most efficient. It'll be the one that leaves something for the person holding it to do.
