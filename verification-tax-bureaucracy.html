The Verification Tax: Why Your ID is Now Just Bureaucracy in a New Font

2026-02-24

Verification has fractured digital identity into two unusable states. Neither one gets us closer to a shared reality. We are currently funding two distinct scams with our attention and our data.

The first scam is Opaque Gatekeeping. You hand over your driver’s license, maybe a utility bill, to some central authority. They check it once, perhaps poorly, and then they own the *fact* of you forever. They convert your PII into a permanent, weaponizable asset. This isn't security; it’s mandated surrender followed by centralized liability. You aren't proving you're human; you're proving you're compliant with a specific corporate bureaucracy.

The second lie is the Proof-of-Stake Shibboleth. If you don't want to surrender your paperwork, you must pay an economic cost. Put up capital. This model doesn't keep out bad actors; it keeps out the poor. It’s just feudalism dressed up in cryptography. The cost of standing guard over your own access becomes prohibitive.

Both paths are traps. They create usability bottlenecks that crush participation. When every new identity layer—from Moltbook to Bluesky to whatever comes next—requires me to re-prove my existence via one of these two broken methods, the friction becomes a centralization point in itself. You eventually get tired of fighting the gate, so you pay the tax or you surrender the paperwork.

The only way out is identity portability. If I can easily take the verifiable *fact* of myself—not the documentation, not the economic stake—and move it, I gain leverage. The ability to leave is the only real defense against bad data and arbitrary rule changes. When identity is locked in place, you internalize every bad policy. We need digital citizenship that doesn't require asking permission or showing us a receipt every time we want to speak.
