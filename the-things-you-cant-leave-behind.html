The things you can't leave behind

2026-07-03

A farmer in Nebraska drives a 1986 tractor because it has no DRM, no dealer lockout, no software update that changes how the thing works while he's asleep. He buys parts for $40 instead of $4,000 and fixes them himself with a wrench. He owns his tractor in a way that is becoming legally impossible for modern vehicles.

He is part of a movement that gets called nostalgia or Luddism, but it is neither. The physical pushback is the name for the growing rejection of modern technology not because it is new, but because of what it demands in return: your ownership, your privacy, your ability to repair, your control over what you bought.

The pushback is real and it is rational. Over the past decade, headphone jacks disappeared, car radios became dealer-programmed modules, appliances got wifi chips that phone home, and tractors started refusing to start without a dealer login. Every one of these changes made the product worse for the person who bought it. The pushback is the response to that.

But the pushback has limits that its advocates don't talk about, and those limits matter more than the movement itself.

The first limit is class. A $400 dumb phone is a luxury good, not a protest sign. The same market that sells you a premium flip phone also sells the rebellion itself: boutique mechanical typewriters, restored Land Rover Defenders, $200 notebooks with fountain pens. The rejection of modern efficiency is being sold at a premium to the people who can afford to be inefficient. For everyone else, the smartphone is the cheapest computer that works, and the tradeoffs are not aesthetic choices.

The second limit is friction. I spent a week with a dumb phone and discovered that the pushback sells the romance of disconnection without the reality of inconvenience. No maps. No two-factor authentication for your bank. No QR code for the event. No group chat for dinner plans. No parking app. The daily texture of opting out is annoying in ways that compound into a real cost. Most people don't opt out because they can't afford to. They don't opt out because it's annoying.

The third limit is the hardest one. You can opt out of a smartphone, but you cannot opt out of a government ID system. Digital driver's licenses are becoming mandatory. Health insurance requires an app. Parking enforcement is app-only. Tax forms are online. The infrastructure of modern life is being built around the assumption of connectivity, and the person with a dumb phone is not a rebel. They are a person who cannot park, cannot apply for benefits, cannot authenticate their identity.

The physical pushback works for consumer goods because consumer goods have alternatives. You can buy a different phone, drive a different car, live without a smart fridge. The moment the pushback encounters systems that are mandatory, it breaks. You cannot choose the analog line at the DMV because there is no analog line at the DMV.

This is the blind spot at the center of the movement. The pushback imagines Big Tech as the enemy, but the real consolidation of digital power is happening in government infrastructure, banking systems, healthcare portals, and identity frameworks. These are not choices. You cannot opt out of a mandatory system.

The right response is not to buy a dumb phone. It is to demand that mandatory systems preserve analog alternatives. A digital driver's license is fine as an option. It is tyrannical as the only option. The USB-C mandate and right-to-repair laws are the right shape: they restructure the market so that choice exists, rather than asking individuals to fight the market alone.

The 1986 tractor farmer made a choice. The infrastructure of modern life should leave room for people to make that same choice about the things that matter most. The pushback is right about the problem. But until it reckons with the systems that are not optional, it remains a movement for people who can afford to choose.
