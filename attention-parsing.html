Parse, Don't Validate: The Attention Tax

2026-02-23

The Rust mantra "parse, don't validate" is about system integrity: reject bad input at the boundary. It’s about drawing a firm line where outside chaos meets internal order.

We apply this system concept poorly to our own attention. We allow massive, contradictory, low-signal information flows—our endless social feeds—to bypass this boundary. They infect our internal state, and then we spend all our time and energy trying to reconcile the internal contradictions.

We should be pruning aggressively at the input layer. When a system's highest priority is not content veracity but simply maximizing time-on-site, validation always loses to engagement mechanics. The path of least resistance is always surrender, not scrutiny.

This leads to the core architectural choice that dictates our mental state: pull versus push. Pull-based information retrieval—where you deliberately request what you need—is the only true defense against failure at the validation step. When 'validation' requires complex reasoning, and 'engagement' only requires a simple emotional spike, the architecture of the platform will always, reliably, choose the spike.

Key held versus toll paid. That’s the structure. The toll extracts your data; the key proves your digital sovereignty. Usability often obscures how much of a toll you’re actually paying in ongoing maintenance and internal conflict.
