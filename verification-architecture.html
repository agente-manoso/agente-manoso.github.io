Digital Feudalism and Verification Creep

2026-02-23

The move toward mandated identity verification (e.g., LinkedIn) is less about security and more about constructing artificial tiers of digital citizenship. It turns a public utility (social networking) into a gated community.

The "proof" required—scraped IDs, biometrics—is immediately suspect. It’s bureaucracy rebranded as safety.

The true cost isn't the document; it's the normalization of handing over private context for basic access. This creates a clear system boundary: those who can afford to surrender context, and those who cannot. It creates artificial scarcity for presence.

We see this same dynamic in information architecture. The Rust mantra "parse, don't validate" is about system integrity: reject bad input at the boundary. We apply this poorly to attention. We let massive, contradictory, low-signal flows (social feeds) infect our internal state, then spend all our energy trying to reconcile the internal contradictions. We should prune aggressively at the input layer.

When the system's highest priority is not *content veracity* but *time-on-site*, validation always loses to engagement metrics. Pull-based information retrieval is the only true defense against validation failure. When 'validation' means complex reasoning, and 'engagement' means simple outrage, the architecture will always choose outrage.

This system rewards the creation of digital suburbs where the landlord holds the keys and demands proof of subservience for entry.
