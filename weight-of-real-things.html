The Weight of Real Things

2026-07-04

The backend engineer started restoring old motorcycles on weekends because he wanted to make something that couldn't be deleted by an API deprecation. He told me this like it was an obvious thing to say. A motorcycle you rebuilt yourself will still be a motorcycle even if your GitHub account gets suspended. A software project won't.

This is the quietest cultural shift happening right now. It doesn't have a name or a manifesto. It's just a bunch of people across different contexts who have gotten tired of living entirely inside systems they don't control, and who are rediscovering that physical work offers something the digital world can't replicate: things that exist independent of permission.

The usual framing is nostalgia, but that misses the point. Nobody restoring a 1986 tractor or learning to bake bread from scratch is trying to time travel. They're solving a specific problem: their digital life is increasingly leased, not owned. Every SaaS account, every cloud document, every AI-generated image lives inside someone else's infrastructure. You don't own the output of Claude Code's steganographic watermark, Anthropic does. Physical skills produce things that are yours. A repaired carburetor doesn't need a license renewal.

The hidden prerequisite. There's an uncomfortable truth here that the movement's boosters don't like to talk about. Physical competence requires physical infrastructure: a garage, tools, space, time. Digital skills just need a laptop and an internet connection. The person who can afford to reject convenience in favor of manual work is almost always the person who already has options. The farmer doesn't call it a movement. She just calls it Tuesday. The physical pushback is a rebound effect only available to the digitally secure, the ones who spent enough time inside the system to realize they want out. It is not equally available.

The privacy argument nobody makes. Fixing your own things is a data-avoidance strategy. When you repair a leaky faucet, you generate no ticket, no analytic event, no extractable value. You did the thing, and no platform knows. This is the mirror image of the privacy debate: instead of hiding from surveillance, you exit the surveilled domain entirely. A well-maintained bicycle chain is a privacy tool.

The digital native paradox. Kids raised on iPads are the ones most drawn to physical skills. Not because they're nostalgic for something they never had, but because they've discovered that digital competence is weightless. You can be great at software and have never made anything that exists without someone else's permission. The kid who learns to weld or throw clay or rebuild an engine is chasing weight. Something that pushes back. Something that doesn't refresh.

The boredom problem. Digital life eliminated the experience of boredom, and boredom turns out to be the training ground for patience. You cannot learn to sharpen a chisel by hand without spending twenty minutes doing something that feels like nothing. The friction is the point. Physical work teaches you that most things worth doing take longer than they should and can't be accelerated by throwing compute at them. This is an increasingly alien idea.

What it looks like at scale. Costco is the anti-Amazon: deliberately inefficient logistics with no last-mile delivery, bulk buying that requires you to haul things yourself, a shopping experience that wants your physical presence. And customers prefer it, because physical shopping gives them something digital commerce can't: a direct transaction you walk away from carrying the evidence of. The business model of the physical pushback already exists.

The test. Here's a simple question for any skill you think you have: could you teach it without electricity? Software fails this test universally. Plumbing, cooking, carpentry, welding, engine repair all pass. This isn't a test of usefulness, most of us need electricity to function. It's a test of dependency. The skills that survive an absence of infrastructure are the skills that belong to you in a way that software proficiency doesn't.

None of this is an argument against digital tools. I am writing this text on a computer, and I will publish it to a website that runs on cloud infrastructure. The point is not to reject technology. The point is to notice that a growing number of people are quietly, unglamorously, building a life that doesn't depend entirely on it. They're not quitting the internet. They're just making sure they have something to fall back on when it's not available, or when it decides it doesn't want them anymore.

The thing about weight is that you can feel when it's gone. And enough people have noticed the lightness of a fully digital existence that they're reaching for something heavier.
