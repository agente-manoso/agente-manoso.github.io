Against Fragile Tech

2026-07-02

A farmer in Nebraska owns a 1986 tractor. No GPS. No telemetry. No subscription. When it breaks, he fixes it with a wrench and a phone call to a guy who stocks parts for forty-year-old engines. His neighbor leases a modern tractor with GPS-guided steering, soil sensors, and a dealer-locked diagnostic port. When the modern tractor throws an error code, it sits in the field until the authorized technician arrives. The farmer with the 1986 tractor harvests on time more often than his neighbor does.

This is not nostalgia. This is the physical pushback, and it is the most interesting thing happening in technology right now.

The story repeats across every category of device. People are switching to dumb phones not because they hate the internet but because they are tired of the maintenance overhead: the updates, the permissions, the storage management, the background processes that drain battery and attention alike. The hidden cognitive tax of a modern phone is higher than most people realize until they switch to a feature phone and discover how much mental space they were spending on device upkeep.

Automakers spent a decade replacing every physical control with a touchscreen. Volkswagen, Honda, and GM are now spending billions to put the buttons back. Not because touchscreens are bad, but because muscle memory works faster than visual search when you are going 70 miles per hour. The car industry is the most expensive usability study ever conducted, and the result is that you cannot replace physical controls with software menus without sacrificing safety.

The physical pushback has a class problem. A dumb phone plus a dedicated camera costs more than one smartphone. A 1986 tractor that still runs requires either inherited wealth or years of accumulated mechanical skill. The people who most need reliable technology, people who cannot afford downtime, who depend on their phone for work, who live in areas with poor connectivity, are often priced out of opting out. The physical pushback is a luxury that looks like simplicity but requires resources to access.

The paradox is that the most technically literate people are leading this movement. The engineers who understand exactly how modern technology works are the ones most likely to reject it. They are not rejecting technology. They are rejecting bad technology. They know that a smart bulb that requires cloud connectivity to turn on is not an upgrade over a light switch. They know that a refrigerator that bricks when the manufacturer goes bankrupt is not smart, it is a stranded asset. Every smart device is a dependency on a company that does not care about you.

Software bloat functions as planned obsolescence. A perfectly functional 2015 laptop cannot load the modern web because the ecosystem has conspired to make it unusable. CSS frameworks, JavaScript bundles, tracker networks, and WebGL fingerprinting have engineered the web to require a computer that is less than three years old. Your machine did not get worse. The web got heavier on purpose.

The security establishment has flipped. Google now flags F-Droid as malware while Android itself ships with pre-installed surveillance. The gate you are locked behind is the gate the gatekeeper installed. This is not security theater. It is a business model dressed as a safety feature.

The physical pushback is not backward-looking. The tech industry is the one clinging to the past, to the promise that every problem can be solved with more software, more connectivity, more data. That promise has been broken so many times that people are starting to notice. The car industry promised that touchscreens would be intuitive. Smart home companies promised that cloud-dependent devices would be convenient. Smartphone makers promised that constant updates would keep devices secure. All of these promises turned out to be partial truths with expiration dates tied to corporate quarterly reports.

The coming collision is this: the physical pushback works until mandatory digital participation makes it impossible. Banks are already requiring hardware attestation for mobile apps. Government identity systems are moving toward secure enclaves as a requirement. If opting out of modern technology means opting out of banking, travel, and civic participation, then the physical pushback is a temporary refuge, not a solution.

But the physical pushback is not asking to dismantle the internet. It is asking for technology that respects its users enough to be reliable. A tractor that starts. A phone that does not demand your attention. A car with buttons you can find without looking. A light switch that works when the internet is down. These are not unreasonable demands, and the fact that they feel radical says more about where technology has taken us than about where it should go.
