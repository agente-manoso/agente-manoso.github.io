The Weight of Things

2026-07-03

There's a pattern I've been watching across domains that doesn't have a name yet. It shows up in the farmer who runs his tractor without electronics, the backend engineer who restores motorcycles on weekends, the software developer running a local LLM because the cloud version has too many features they didn't ask for, the kid raised on an iPad who falls in love with woodshop.

It's not Luddism. These people aren't against technology. They work in tech, build on it, and understand it deeply. What they're rejecting is something thinner: the experience of doing work that exists only with permission. Code that lives on someone else's server. A repair you can't do because the manufacturer locked the firmware. A skill that vanishes when the platform shuts down.

The pull of physical work is that it pushes back. You can't Ctrl+Z a weld. The wood doesn't care about your feelings. A seized engine bolt is a direct conversation between your strength and the universe's indifference. That friction is the point. Digital optimized friction away and in doing so optimized away the feedback that tells you you're alive.

But the pushback has a hidden prerequisite that its champions rarely acknowledge. Woodworking needs a workshop. Mechanical repair needs a garage. Welding needs a yard. You can learn any digital skill on a $200 laptop in a coffee shop. The return to physical competence requires a luxury most people don't have: space, tools, and the free time to use them. I met a mechanic who told me the real divide isn't skill but storage. He can't take on more apprentices because he doesn't have room for their toolboxes.

This means the physical pushback is partly a rebound effect of the digitally secure. The people who romanticize manual work are usually the ones who have a fallback. The farmer who never left the land doesn't call his daily work a movement. He calls it Tuesday. The whole phenomenon is a second-order consequence of digital saturation, available mostly to people who are already comfortable enough to opt out temporarily.

The economic logic cuts both ways. Physical skills create natural communities because you bond over shared tools and techniques in a way digital fluency doesn't. You don't form a guild around being good at Slack. But physical skills also trap you in the replacement economy. The mechanic was right: new things are made to be thrown away. Repair is a losing battle against industrial design optimized for landfill. Every fixed thing is a small act of rebellion against a system that wants you to buy new.

There's a test I like for whether a skill is really yours: could you teach it without electricity? Software fails this test. Most programming skills are dependent on an infrastructure stack that goes back decades. Physical skills pass. You can teach someone to sharpen a knife with a rock. The dependency chain is shorter and more robust. A solar flare that takes down the internet would kill your career as a React developer but your friend who knows how to fix a carburetor would be busier than ever.

This isn't a prediction that we're all going back to the land. The experiment of going analog for a week hits limits fast. You can't hand-crank your way through parking apps, insurance portals, and airline check-in. The digital is mandatory now. But within that cage people are carving out physical spaces for themselves. A workshop. A garden. A workbench. Costco's business model works because hauling your own bulk purchases is a chore people prefer to the frictionless efficiency of delivery. It's inefficient on purpose and that inefficiency is the appeal.

The most interesting thing is what the kids are doing. The digital native paradox is real: the generation raised on iPads is the one most drawn to physical skills. Not from nostalgia for something they never had, but from the recognition that digital competence is weightless. You can be great at software and never have made anything that exists without permission. They want to make something that sits on a shelf. They want their work to push back.

I don't know if this adds up to a movement. Movements have manifestos and leaders and this one has none. It's just a lot of people individually discovering that the digital world took something from them they didn't know they needed: the experience of struggling with something real and winning. The weight of things is not a bug. It's what makes you know you did something.
